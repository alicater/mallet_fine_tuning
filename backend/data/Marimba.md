The **marimba** ([/məˈrɪmbə/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English") [_mə-RIM-bə_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")) is a musical instrument in the [percussion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percussion "Percussion") family that consists of wooden bars that are struck by [mallets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mallet "Mallet"). Below each bar is a resonator pipe that amplifies particular harmonics of its sound. Compared to the [xylophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylophone "Xylophone"), the marimba has a lower range. Typically, the bars of a marimba are arranged chromatically, like the keys of a piano. The marimba is a type of [idiophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiophone "Idiophone").

Today, the marimba is used as a solo instrument, or in ensembles like [orchestras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orchestra "Orchestra"), [marching bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marching_band "Marching band") (typically as a part of the [front ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Front_ensemble "Front ensemble")), [percussion ensembles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percussion_ensemble "Percussion ensemble"), [brass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brass_band "Brass band") and [concert bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concert_band "Concert band"), and other traditional ensembles.

## Etymology and terminology

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Instruments_de_musique_des_Congolais%2C_gravure_extraite_de_Girolamo_Merolla_da_Sorrento.jpg/250px-Instruments_de_musique_des_Congolais%2C_gravure_extraite_de_Girolamo_Merolla_da_Sorrento.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Instruments_de_musique_des_Congolais,_gravure_extraite_de_Girolamo_Merolla_da_Sorrento.jpg)

An array of named instruments in the [Kongo Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kongo "Kingdom of Kongo") by Girolamo Merolla da Sorrento (1692)

The term _marimba_ refers to both the traditional version of this instrument and its modern form. Its first documented use in the English language dates back to 1704.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-1) The term is of [Bantu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bantu_languages "Bantu languages") origin, deriving from the prefix _ma-_ meaning 'many' and _-rimba_ meaning 'xylophone'. The term is akin to [Kikongo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kongo_languages "Kongo languages") and [Swahili](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swahili_language "Swahili language") _marimba_ or _malimba_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-2)

## History

### Ancient

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Women_with_Water-Pots%2C_Listening_to_the_Music_of_the_Marimba%2C_Sansa%2C_and_Pan%E2%80%99s_Pipes.jpg/250px-Women_with_Water-Pots%2C_Listening_to_the_Music_of_the_Marimba%2C_Sansa%2C_and_Pan%E2%80%99s_Pipes.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Women_with_Water-Pots,_Listening_to_the_Music_of_the_Marimba,_Sansa,_and_Pan%E2%80%99s_Pipes.jpg)

Marimba players in Africa

Instruments like the marimba are present throughout the entirety of [sub-Saharan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa "Sub-Saharan Africa") Africa.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-3) The instrument itself is most similar and shares its name with the marimbas of modern-day [Angola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angola "Angola") and the [Democratic Republic of the Congo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo "Democratic Republic of the Congo").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-4) However, it is also similar to instruments that exist in [West Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa") such as the [balafon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balafon "Balafon") of the [Mandinka people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandinka_people "Mandinka people"), known as [gyil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyil "Gyil") among the [Gur peoples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gur_people "Gur people") in and around northern [Ghana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ghana "Ghana") and [Burkina Faso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burkina_Faso "Burkina Faso").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-5)

### Mexico

The first known dates back to 1545 in the Santa Lucía hacienda, in the municipality of [Jiquipilas, Chiapas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jiquipilas,_Chiapas "Jiquipilas, Chiapas"). According to documentation dated October 9, 1545, the encomendero Don Pedro Gentil de Bustamante and owner of the hacienda of Santa Lucia, describes in his chronicle a marimba in a celebration of Indians; and tells us the following:.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-7)

> "...said instrument is composed of eight slats of red wood, unequal in size, which are pierced together with cord and produce joyful echoes with boards of the macaguil (macagüil) stick (...) rows of boards tied to short orchestras (sic) flanged and stretched under said instrument a hole in the ground and glued with resin on the boards snake rattles that make musical vibrate with blows of two small sticks with black wax head one for each hand... ...that this music is accompanied by a drum of animal skin (...)"[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-Fray_Antonio_1-8)

It is believed that xylophones came to America by means of Africans who had been taken to Guatemala and Mexico, although there are also records in some Mayan pyramids found in Chiapas and Guatemala. The first documented mention of the marimba in Guatemala (marimba de tecomates), dates from November 13, 1680 during the inauguration celebrations of the Santa Iglesia Catedral in Santiago de los Caballeros de Guatemala.

The modern double keyboard marimba was created in 1892, in Chiapas, Mexico, thanks to the innovation of [Corazón de Jesús Borras Moreno](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coraz%C3%B3n_de_Jes%C3%BAs_Borras_Moreno&action=edit&redlink=1 "Corazón de Jesús Borras Moreno (page does not exist)"), a native of the Municipality of [Venustiano Carranza](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Venustiano_Carranza_\(Chiapas\)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Venustiano Carranza (Chiapas) (page does not exist)"), Chiapas, Mexico. In 1897, the current model of marimba was played for the first time in the traditional park of the church of Señor del Pozo, in the same municipality, from that moment it has gone from being a native instrument to a concert instrument.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-9) A five-octave instrument was first built, and later an 11-octave, huge instrument was built, which was played by 9 elements.

In Central America there are two versions of its origin. Some claim that it was brought by black slaves from Africa, while others consider it native, created by the Maya-quiche.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-10)

Historian David Vela says: "We also refer to the thesis of Dr. Castañeda Paganini on the possible invention of the marimba in Guatemala, by Africans brought as slaves in the sixteenth century; it is surprising however that the marimba appears here early among communities closed to their influence, among remote mountains, and is missing in the areas actually inhabited by the colored race." what is undoubted is that the ingenuity of the local countries transformed the instrument to the point of making it their own.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-11)

Since there are records in Guatemala that in the middle of the 18th century, in the same city of Santiago de Guatemala (today Antigua Guatemala), the presbyter Joseph de Padilla developed a new version of the instrument (simple marimba), to which he extended the extension of the keyboard to 42 keys (first and only collective instrument in the world), he added a structure with 4 legs raising it from the ground, being able to play standing up.

In 1894 in Quetzaltenango, the master Sebastián Hurtado developed the first chromatic marimba or double keyboard marimba made of Hormigo wood (Plathymiscium dimorphandrum) giving it up to 6 musical scales. He inherited the marimba to his sons and they created the group Marimba Royal of the Hurtado brothers who in 1908 performed a concert in the city of Buffalo, New York, and thus introduced the marimba (with the contributions of Guatemala) in the United States, making it known to the world.

Being an instrument used in many countries of the Americas, on February 12, 2015, the Organization of American States [(OAS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organization_of_American_States "Organization of American States") declares the marimba "Cultural Heritage of the Americas".

### Central America

See also: [Music of Guatemala § Marimba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_Guatemala#Marimba "Music of Guatemala")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/The_Marimba_%281888%29.jpg/250px-The_Marimba_%281888%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Marimba_\(1888\).jpg)

"The Marimba" from "The Capitals of Spanish America" (1888)

The marimba is popular throughout [Central America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_America "Central America"), with its popularity spreading from southern Mexico to [Costa Rica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costa_Rica "Costa Rica"). The first historical account in Central America is from 1550 where enslaved Africans in [Guatemala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guatemala "Guatemala") are reported playing it. By 1680 accounts of [Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_peoples "Maya peoples") musicians using marimbas with [gourd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gourd "Gourd") resonator were made in [Guatemala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guatemala "Guatemala"). It became more widespread during the 18th and 19th centuries, as Maya and [Ladino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladino_people "Ladino people") ensembles started using it on festivals. In 1821, the marimba was proclaimed the national instrument of Guatemala in its independence proclamation.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-Garland-12)

### South America

See also: [Music of Colombia § Pacific Region of Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_Colombia#Pacific_Region_of_Colombia "Music of Colombia"), and [Music of Ecuador § Afro-Ecuadorian music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_of_Ecuador#Afro-Ecuadorian_music "Music of Ecuador")

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Manuel_Mar%C3%ADa_Paz_%28watercolor_9040%2C_1853_CE%29.png/250px-Manuel_Mar%C3%ADa_Paz_%28watercolor_9040%2C_1853_CE%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Manuel_Mar%C3%ADa_Paz_\(watercolor_9040,_1853_CE\).png)

A watercolor of marimbas by [Manuel María Paz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_Mar%C3%ADa_Paz "Manuel María Paz") from the Province of [Barbacoas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barbacoas,_Nari%C3%B1o "Barbacoas, Nariño") in [Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombia "Colombia") (1853)

Marimba's second range of popularity in [Latin America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America") is in the [Pacific coast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_coast "Pacific coast") of Colombia and Ecuador.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-13) The instruments were brought there via the [African diaspora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_diaspora "African diaspora") and their cultural significance has survived to the present day.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-Kraul-14) The [Afro-Latino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro%E2%80%93Latin_Americans "Afro–Latin Americans") communities that take part in preserving and playing it value its importance as a touchstone of their resilience.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-15)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Colombian_Police_%286904573755%29.jpg/250px-Colombian_Police_%286904573755%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Colombian_Police_\(6904573755\).jpg)

[Afro-Colombian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Colombian "Afro-Colombian") youth playing the _marimba de chonta_

In [Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombia "Colombia") the most widespread marimba is the _marimba de chonta_ ([peach-palm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bactris_gasipaes "Bactris gasipaes") marimba). Marimba music has been listed on [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") as an intangible part of Colombian culture.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-16) In recent times _marimberos_ (marimba players) and the marimba genres as a whole have started to fade out in popularity.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-Kraul-14) Nonetheless, the genre is still popular in the departments of [Chocó](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department "Chocó Department") and [Cauca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauca_Department "Cauca Department").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-17)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Esmeraldian_%28Afro-Ecuadorian%29_marimba.jpg/250px-Esmeraldian_%28Afro-Ecuadorian%29_marimba.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Esmeraldian_\(Afro-Ecuadorian\)_marimba.jpg)

An example of the [Afro-Ecuadorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Ecuadorian "Afro-Ecuadorian") _marimba esmeraldeña_

In [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador") the most widespread marimba is the _marimba esmeraldeña_ ([Esmeralda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esmeraldas,_Ecuador "Esmeraldas, Ecuador") marimba).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-:0-18) Marimbas are an important aspect of Afro-Ecuadorian culture: many religious ceremonies and songs are accompanied with marimba music along with festivals and dances.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-:0-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-19) It is most popular in the province of Esmeraldas where in the 16th century [Alonso de Illescas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alonso_de_Illescas "Alonso de Illescas"), a [maroon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maroons "Maroons"), found a [maroon settlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quilombo "Quilombo") near the area around modern day Esmeraldas. In that province, it evokes a sense of pride for the community in which years centuries marimba music has been prohibited after government encroachment upon the [Esmeraldas province](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esmeraldas_Province "Esmeraldas Province").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-20)

### Modern

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/MarimbaTimeOnFrenchmenMardiGras2009.JPG/250px-MarimbaTimeOnFrenchmenMardiGras2009.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MarimbaTimeOnFrenchmenMardiGras2009.JPG)

A marimba player on the streets of [New Orleans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Orleans "New Orleans"), [Louisiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana "Louisiana"), during [Mardi Gras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mardi_Gras_in_New_Orleans "Mardi Gras in New Orleans")

Marimbas have become widely popular around the world since it was being used throughout [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia"), [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America"), [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America") and [Central America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_America "Central America").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-21)

In 1850, Mexican marimbist Manuel Bolán Cruz (1810–1863), modified the old bow marimba, by the wooden straight one, lengthening the legs so that the musicians could play in a standing mode, expanded the keyboard and replaced the gourd resonators by wooden boxes.

In 1892, Mexican musician [Corazón de Jesús Borras Moreno](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coraz%C3%B3n_de_Jes%C3%BAs_Borras_Moreno&action=edit&redlink=1 "Corazón de Jesús Borras Moreno (page does not exist)") [[es](https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coraz%C3%B3n_de_Jes%C3%BAs_Borras_Moreno "es:Corazón de Jesús Borras Moreno")] expanded the range of the marimba to include the [chromatic scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromatic_scale "Chromatic scale") by adding another row of sound bars, akin to black keys on the piano.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-22)

The name _marimba_ was later applied to the orchestra instrument inspired by the Latin American model. In the United States, companies like [J.C. Deagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J.C._Deagan,_Inc. "J.C. Deagan, Inc.") and the [Leedy Manufacturing Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leedy_Manufacturing_Company "Leedy Manufacturing Company") adapted the Latin American instruments for use in Western music. Metal tubes were used as resonators, fine-tuned by rotating metal discs at the bottom; lowest note tubes were U-shaped. The marimbas were first used for light music and dance, such as [vaudeville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaudeville "Vaudeville") theater and comedy shows. [Clair Omar Musser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clair_Omar_Musser "Clair Omar Musser") was a chief proponent of marimba in the United States at the time.

In 1940, the American composer [Paul Creston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Creston "Paul Creston") wrote the first composition for solo marimba (_Concertino for Marimba and Orchestra_).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-23) French composer [Darius Milhaud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_Milhaud "Darius Milhaud") also helped introduce marimbas into [Western classical music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_classical_music "Western classical music") with his 1947 _Concerto for Marimba and Vibraphone_. Four-mallet grip was employed to play [chords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_\(music\) "Chord (music)"), enhancing interest for the instrument.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-Vienna-24) In the late 20th century, [modernist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernism_\(music\) "Modernism (music)") and [contemporary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contemporary_classical_music "Contemporary classical music") composers found new ways to use marimba: notable examples include [Leoš Janáček](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo%C5%A1_Jan%C3%A1%C4%8Dek "Leoš Janáček") (_[Jenufa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jenufa "Jenufa")_), [Carl Orff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Orff "Carl Orff") (_[Antigonae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigonae "Antigonae")_), [Karl Amadeus Hartmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Amadeus_Hartmann "Karl Amadeus Hartmann"), [Hans Werner Henze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Werner_Henze "Hans Werner Henze") (_[Elegy for Young Lovers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elegy_for_Young_Lovers "Elegy for Young Lovers")_), [Pierre Boulez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Boulez "Pierre Boulez") (_[Le marteau sans maître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_marteau_sans_ma%C3%AEtre "Le marteau sans maître")_) and [Steve Reich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Reich "Steve Reich").

### Bars

Marimba bars may be made either of wood or a synthetic [fiberglass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiberglass "Fiberglass") material. For the best sound quality, [rosewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosewood "Rosewood") is the most desirable, while [padauk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padauk "Padauk") is a popular affordable alternative. Synthetic fiberglass bars are often sold under trade names such as Kelon (for [Ludwig-Musser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig-Musser "Ludwig-Musser")), Klyperon (for [J.C. Deagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J.C._Deagan,_Inc. "J.C. Deagan, Inc.")), or Acoustalon (for [Yamaha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamaha_Corporation "Yamaha Corporation")), among others. Bars made from synthetic materials generally fall short in sound quality and generally have a longer decay in comparison to wooden bars, but they are often less expensive and yield added durability and weather resistance, making them suitable for outdoor use. For wooden bars, changes in humidity or temperature may alter the moisture levels within the wood. This may negatively affect the pitch and tonality of the bar.

[Bubinga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubinga "Bubinga") (_Guibourtia demeusei_) and [mahogany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahogany "Mahogany") have also been cited as comparable to rosewood in quality for use as marimba bars. The specific rosewood, _[Dalbergia stevensonii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalbergia_stevensonii "Dalbergia stevensonii")_, only grows in Southern Guatemala and Belize, formerly British Honduras.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-25) This wood has a [Janka rating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Janka_hardness_test "Janka hardness test") of 2200, which is about three times harder than [silver maple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_maple "Silver maple"). The bars are wider and longer at the lowest-pitched notes, and gradually get narrower and shorter as the notes get higher. During the tuning, wood is taken from the middle underside of the bar to lower the pitch. Because of this, the bars are also thinner in the lowest pitch register and thicker in the highest pitch register. While most American marimbas are tuned to the standard A4=440 Hz, many commercial marimbas are tuned to A4=442 Hz for a brighter sound for better blend with an orchestra.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-26)

Marimba bars produce their fullest sound when struck just off center, while striking the bar in the center produces a more articulate tone. On chromatic marimbas, the accidentals can also be played on the extreme front edge of the bar, away from the [node](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Node_\(physics\) "Node (physics)") (the place where the string goes through the bar) if necessary. Playing on the node produces a sonically weak tone, and the technique is only used when the player or composer is looking for a muted sound from the instrument.

### Range

There is no standard range of the marimba, but the most common ranges are 4.3 octaves, 4.5 octaves and 5 octaves; 4, 4.6 and 5.5 octave sizes are also available.

- 4 octave: C3 to C7.
- 4.3 octave: A2 to C7. The .3 refers to three semitones below the 4 octave instrument. This is the most common range.
- 4.5 octave: F2 to C7. The .5 means "half";
- 4.6 octave: E2 to C7, one semitone below the 4.5. Useful for playing guitar literature and transcriptions.
- 5 octave: C2 to C7, one full octave below the 4 octave instrument, useful for playing cello transcriptions, e.g., [J. S. Bach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._S._Bach "J. S. Bach")'s cello suites.
- Bass range (varies, but examples range from G1–G3 or C2–F3)

The range of the marimba has been gradually expanding, with companies like Marimba One adding notes up to F above the normal high C (C7) on their 5.5 octave instrument and marimba tuners adding notes lower than the low C on the 5 octave C2. Adding lower notes is somewhat impractical; as the bars become bigger and the resonators become longer, the instrument must be taller and the mallets must be softer in order to produce a tone rather than just a percussive attack. Adding higher notes is also impractical because the hardness of the mallets required to produce the characteristic tone of a marimba are much too hard to play with in almost any other, lower range on the instrument.

The marimba is a non-transposing instrument with no octave displacement, unlike the [xylophone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylophone "Xylophone"), which sounds one octave higher than written, and the [glockenspiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glockenspiel "Glockenspiel"), which sounds two octaves higher than written.

### Resonators

Part of the key to the marimba's rich sound are its [resonators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonator "Resonator"). These are tubes (usually [aluminum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminum "Aluminum")) that hang below each bar.

In the most traditional versions, various sizes of natural [gourds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gourd "Gourd") are attached below the keys to act as resonators; in more sophisticated versions carved wooden resonators are substituted, allowing for more precise tuning of pitch. In Central America and Mexico, a hole is often carved into the bottom of each resonator and then covered with a delicate membrane taken from the intestine of a pig to add a characteristic "buzzing" or "rattling" sound known as _charleo_.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-27) In more contemporary-style marimbas, wood is replaced by [PVC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride") tubing. The holes in the bottoms of the tubes are covered with a thin layer of paper to produce the buzzing noise.

The length of the resonators varies according to the frequency that the bar produces. Vibrations from the bars resonate as they pass through the tubes, which amplify the tone in a manner very similar to the way in which the body of a guitar or cello would. In instruments exceeding 4+1⁄2 octaves, the length of tubing required for the [bass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bass_\(sound\) "Bass (sound)") notes exceeds the height of the instrument. Some manufacturers, such as [DeMorrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=DeMorrow&action=edit&redlink=1 "DeMorrow (page does not exist)") and [Malletech](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Malletech&action=edit&redlink=1 "Malletech (page does not exist)"), compensate for this by bending the ends of the tubes. This involves soldering smaller straight sections of tubes to form "curved" tubes. Both DeMorrow and Malletech use brass rather than aluminium. Others, such as [Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adams_Musical_Instruments "Adams Musical Instruments") and [Yamaha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yamaha_\(manufacturer\) "Yamaha (manufacturer)"), expand the tubes into large box-shaped bottoms, resulting in the necessary amount of resonating space without having to extend the tubes. This result is achieved by the custom manufacturer Marimba One by widening the resonators into an oval shape, with the lowest ones reaching nearly a foot in width, and doubling the tube up inside the lowest resonators—a process known as ["Haskelling"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haskell_organ_pipe_construction "Haskell organ pipe construction"), originally used in pipe organ resonators, and named for its inventor, [William E. Haskell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_E._Haskell "William E. Haskell").

Resonator tuning involves adjusting "stops" in the tubes themselves to compensate for temperature and humidity conditions in the room where the instrument is stored. Some companies offer adjustment in the upper octaves only. Others do not have any adjustable stops. Still some companies (Malletech and DeMorrow) offer full-range adjustable stops.

On many marimbas, decorative resonators are added to fill the gaps in the accidental resonator bank. In addition to this, the resonator lengths are sometimes altered to form a decorative arch, such as in the Musser M-250. This does not affect the resonant properties, because the end plugs in the resonators are still placed at their respective lengths.

### Mallets

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Miami-Dade_County_Public_Schools_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation_%282%29.jpg/250px-Miami-Dade_County_Public_Schools_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation_%282%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Miami-Dade_County_Public_Schools_-_Flickr_-_Knight_Foundation_\(2\).jpg)

An example of the mallets used when playing a marimba

The mallet shaft is commonly made of wood, usually [birch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch "Birch") or rattan, but may also be made of [fiberglass or carbon fibre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiberglass "Fiberglass"). The most common diameter of the shaft is around 8 mm (5⁄16 in). Shafts made of rattan have a certain elasticity to them, while birch has almost no give. Professionals use both depending on their preferences, whether they are playing with two mallets or more, and which grip they use if they are using a four-mallet grip.

Appropriate mallets for the instrument depend on the range. The material at the end of the shaft is almost always a type of rubber, usually wrapped with yarn. Softer mallets are used at the lowest notes, and harder mallets are used at the highest notes. Mallets that are too hard will damage the instrument, and mallets that might be appropriate for the upper range could damage the notes in the lower range (especially on a [padouk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Padouk "Padouk") or [rosewood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosewood "Rosewood") instrument). On the lower notes, the bars are larger, and require a softer mallet to bring out a strong fundamental. Because of the need to use varying hardnesses of mallets, some players, when playing with four or more mallets, might use graduated mallets to match the bars that they are playing (softer on the left, harder on the right).

Some mallets, called "two-toned" or "multi-tonal", have a hard core, loosely wrapped with yarn. These are designed to sound articulate when playing at a loud dynamic, and broader at the quieter dynamics.

## Mallet technique

Modern marimba music calls for simultaneous use of between two and four mallets (sometimes up to six or eight), granting the performer the ability to play chords or music with large interval skips more easily. Multiple mallets are held in the same hand using any of a number of techniques or "grips". For four mallets (two mallets in each hand), the most common grips are the [Burton grip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burton_grip "Burton grip") (made popular by [Gary Burton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Burton "Gary Burton")), the traditional grip (or "cross grip") and the [Musser-Stevens grip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stevens_technique "Stevens technique") (made popular by [Leigh Howard Stevens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leigh_Howard_Stevens "Leigh Howard Stevens")). Each grip is perceived to have its own benefits and drawbacks. For example, some marimbists feel the Musser-Stevens grip is more suitable for quick interval changes and mallet independence, while the Burton grip is more suitable for stronger playing or switching between chords and single-note melody lines. The traditional grip gives a greater dynamic range and freedom of playing. The choice of grip varies by region (the Musser-Stevens grip and the Burton grip are more popular in the United States, while the traditional grip is more popular in Japan), by instrument (the Burton grip is less likely to be used on marimba than on a [vibraphone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibraphone "Vibraphone")) and by the preference of the individual performer.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-28)

Six-mallet grips consist of variations on these three grips. Six-mallet marimba grips have been used for years by Mexican and Central American marimbists, but they are generally considered non-standard in the Western classical canon. [Keiko Abe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keiko_Abe "Keiko Abe") has written a number of compositions for six mallets, including a section in her concerto _Prism Rhapsody_. Other marimbists/composers using this technique include Rebecca Kite (who commissioned composer [Evan Hause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evan_Hause "Evan Hause") to write _Circe_, a major work for six mallets, in 2001), Dean Gronemeier, [Robert Paterson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Paterson_\(composer\) "Robert Paterson (composer)"), and Kai Stensgaard. Paterson's grip is based on the Burton grip, and his grip and technique have been called the Paterson grip, and even the Wolverine grip. Paterson states that his technique differs from others in that there is less emphasis places on block chords on the lower bank of notes (the naturals or white notes) and more emphasis on independence, one-handed rolls, and alternations between mallets 12-3 or 1–23 in the left hand (or 45-6 or 4–56 in the right hand, respectively), and so on. Ludwig Albert published at first a work for eight mallets and demonstrated the Ludwig Albert eight-mallet grip based on the traditional grip from 1995.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

## Repertoire

A marimba player for the [NDR Radiophilharmonie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NDR_Radiophilharmonie "NDR Radiophilharmonie")

### Orchestra

The marimba is less used by [composers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composer "Composer") than other [keyboards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keyboard_instrument "Keyboard instrument") from [orchestral percussion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orchestral_percussion "Orchestral percussion") section, although its interest increased after 1950 such as in [Le marteau sans maître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Marteau_sans_ma%C3%AEtre "Le Marteau sans maître") by [Pierre Boulez](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Boulez "Pierre Boulez").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-29)

### Concertos

The first solo marimba concerto, Concertino for Marimba, was composed by [Paul Creston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Creston "Paul Creston") in 1940, after a commission by [Frédérique Petrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9rique_Petrides "Frédérique Petrides"). The Concertino for Marimba premiered on 29 April 1940 in [Carnegie Hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnegie_Hall "Carnegie Hall") with marimba soloist [Ruth Stuber Jeanne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruth_Stuber_Jeanne "Ruth Stuber Jeanne") and the [Orchestrette Classique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orchestrette_Classique "Orchestrette Classique").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-30) The second concerto for the marimba, Concerto for Marimba, Vibraphone and Orchestra, was written by [Darius Milhaud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_Milhaud "Darius Milhaud") in 1947.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-31)

The [Oregon Symphony Orchestra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oregon_Symphony_Orchestra "Oregon Symphony Orchestra") commissioned [Tomáš Svoboda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Svoboda_\(composer\) "Tomáš Svoboda (composer)") to compose Concerto for Marimba and Orchestra, Op. 148, in 1995.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-32) A recording of the piece by the orchestra and Niel DePonte was nominated for the 2004 [Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Soloist(s) Performance (with orchestra)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Instrumental_Soloist\(s\)_Performance_\(with_orchestra\) "Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Soloist(s) Performance (with orchestra)").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-33)

Other prominent concertos include Concerto No. 1 for Marimba and String Orchestra written in 1986 by [Ney Rosauro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ney_Rosauro "Ney Rosauro") and Concerto for Marimba and String Orchestra written in 2006 by [Emmanuel Séjourné](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_S%C3%A9journ%C3%A9 "Emmanuel Séjourné").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-34)

### Solos

The marimba is the most popular solo keyboard percussion instrument in classical music. Popular marimba solos range from beginner solos such as _[Yellow After the Rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_After_the_Rain "Yellow After the Rain")_ and _[Sea Refractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sea_Refractions&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sea Refractions (page does not exist)")_ by Mitchell Peters to more advanced works such as _Variations on Lost Love_ by [David Maslanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Maslanka "David Maslanka"), _Rhythmic Caprice_ by [Leigh Howard Stevens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leigh_Howard_Stevens "Leigh Howard Stevens") and _[Khan Variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khan_Variations "Khan Variations")_ by [Alejandro Viñao](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alejandro_Vi%C3%B1ao "Alejandro Viñao").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-35)

## Popular music

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/GuatemalanMarimbaGourds.JPG/250px-GuatemalanMarimbaGourds.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:GuatemalanMarimbaGourds.JPG)

Folk marimba with gourds, Highland [Guatemala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guatemala "Guatemala")

Traditional marimba bands are especially popular in [Guatemala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guatemala "Guatemala"), where they are the national symbol of culture, but are also strongly established in the Mexican states of [Chiapas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiapas "Chiapas"), [Tabasco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabasco "Tabasco"), and [Oaxaca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oaxaca "Oaxaca"). They are also very popular in other Central American nations such as [Honduras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honduras "Honduras"), [El Salvador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/El_Salvador "El Salvador"), [Nicaragua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicaragua "Nicaragua"), and [Costa Rica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costa_Rica "Costa Rica"), as well as among [Afro-Ecuadorians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Ecuadorians "Afro-Ecuadorians") and [Afro-Colombians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afro-Colombians "Afro-Colombians").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-36)

There have been numerous jazz [vibraphonists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibraphone "Vibraphone") who also played the marimba. Notable among them are [Gary Burton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_Burton "Gary Burton"), [David Friedman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Friedman_\(percussionist\) "David Friedman (percussionist)"), [Stefon Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stefon_Harris "Stefon Harris"), [Bobby Hutcherson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bobby_Hutcherson "Bobby Hutcherson"), [Joe Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Locke_\(musician\) "Joe Locke (musician)"), [Steve Nelson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Nelson_\(vibraphonist\) "Steve Nelson (vibraphonist)"), [Red Norvo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Norvo "Red Norvo"), [Dave Pike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Pike "Dave Pike"), [Gloria Parker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gloria_Parker "Gloria Parker"), [Dave Samuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dave_Samuels "Dave Samuels"), and Arthur Lipner.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Marimbist and vibraphonist [Julius Wechter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Wechter "Julius Wechter") was the leader of a popular 1960s Latin-flavored band called [Baja Marimba Band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baja_Marimba_Band "Baja Marimba Band"). [Herb Alpert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herb_Alpert "Herb Alpert") and his [Tijuana Brass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tijuana_Brass "Tijuana Brass") made frequent use of the marimba.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-37)

[Ruth Underwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruth_Underwood "Ruth Underwood") played an electrically amplified marimba in [Frank Zappa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Zappa "Frank Zappa")'s [The Mothers of Invention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mothers_of_Invention "The Mothers of Invention").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marimba#cite_note-38)

[The Rolling Stones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones "The Rolling Stones")' 1966 song "[Under My Thumb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Under_My_Thumb "Under My Thumb")" prominently features a marimba, played by [Brian Jones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Jones "Brian Jones").

A marimba solo was in the hit 1975 [Starbuck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starbuck_\(band\) "Starbuck (band)") song "[Moonlight Feels Right](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight_Feels_Right#Marimba_solo "Moonlight Feels Right")".

The 1982 [Toto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toto_\(band\) "Toto (band)") hit "[Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa_\(song\) "Africa (song)")" prominently features the marimba, played by [Joe Porcaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Porcaro "Joe Porcaro").

The version of the song "[Tonight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonight_\(Iggy_Pop_song\) "Tonight (Iggy Pop song)")" by [David Bowie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Bowie "David Bowie") and [Iggy Pop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iggy_Pop "Iggy Pop") featured on Bowie's 1984 [album of the same title](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonight_\(David_Bowie_album\) "Tonight (David Bowie album)") includes a marimba.

[Peter Gabriel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Gabriel "Peter Gabriel")'s 1985 soundtrack album _[Birdy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birdy_\(Peter_Gabriel_album\) "Birdy (Peter Gabriel album)")_ includes the song "Slow Marimbas."
